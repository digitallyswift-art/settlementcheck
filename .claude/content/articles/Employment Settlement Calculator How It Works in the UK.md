Employment Settlement Calculator: How to Estimate Your UK Offer

You have just been offered a settlement agreement. Before you sign, you want to know if the figure is fair.

An employment settlement calculator does one job. It takes the legal facts of your situation and turns them into a number you can compare against your offer.

This guide explains what a calculator can work out, what it cannot, and how each part of the figure is built up under UK law. Every component below is grounded in the Employment Rights Act 1996 and current HMRC tax rules.

What an employment settlement calculator actually does

A settlement calculator does not predict what an employment tribunal would award. It estimates what you are owed under UK statute, plus a typical market range for the additional compensation that employers offer to settle.

In other words, it gives you two things:

- A statutory floor. The amount you are entitled to under the law before any negotiation begins.
- A typical range. What employees in similar situations usually receive once the negotiable element is added.

If your offer falls below the floor, that is a clear signal. If it falls inside the typical range, it may still be worth negotiating. A solicitor can interpret the gap.

The four building blocks of a UK settlement

Almost every UK settlement agreement is built from the same four parts. Some apply to your situation, some do not.

1. Statutory redundancy pay (if your role is being made redundant)
2. Statutory notice pay (or contractual notice, whichever is longer)
3. Unpaid contractual entitlements (wages, holiday, bonus, commission)
4. An ex gratia payment for loss of employment (the negotiable part)

A good calculator builds your floor from parts one to three, then estimates a fair range for part four.

Part 1: Statutory redundancy pay

Statutory redundancy pay is set out in section 162 of the Employment Rights Act 1996. The formula is fixed. Your length of service, age, and weekly pay determine the amount.

The multiplier per completed year of service depends on your age in that year:

- Under 22: half a week's pay
- 22 to 40: one week's pay
- 41 and over: one and a half week's pay

There is a cap on the weekly pay used in the calculation. The current cap is £719 per week, set under section 227 of the Employment Rights Act and updated on 6 April 2025. Service is capped at 20 years.

If your role is not being made redundant, this part of the calculation does not apply.

Part 2: Statutory notice pay

Section 86 of the Employment Rights Act 1996 sets the minimum notice an employer must give:

- Less than one month of service: zero weeks
- One month to two years: one week
- Two years and over: one week per completed year, capped at twelve weeks

If your contract gives you a longer notice period, your contract takes priority. Notice pay uses your actual weekly pay, with no cap, under section 221 of the Employment Rights Act.

Notice pay is taxable in the same way as your normal salary. Since April 2018, all payments in lieu of notice (PILON) are subject to income tax and National Insurance, regardless of whether the contract has a PILON clause.

Part 3: Unpaid contractual entitlements

Before any negotiation, your employer must settle anything you have already earned:

- Outstanding wages up to your termination date
- Accrued but unused holiday pay
- Earned bonus or commission, depending on your contract terms

These are not gifts. They are money you have already worked for. They should appear as separate line items in the agreement.

Part 4: The ex gratia payment

This is where the negotiation happens. The ex gratia payment is a sum your employer pays in exchange for your agreement not to bring an employment tribunal claim or other legal action.

It is also the part that uses the £30,000 tax-free rule. Under section 403 of the Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003, the first £30,000 of a genuine compensation payment for loss of employment is paid tax-free. Anything above that figure is taxed as income.

The size of a fair ex gratia payment depends on the strength of your underlying position. A useful starting point is the relationship between the ex gratia and your statutory floor:

- A typical low-end offer is around 1.5 times the statutory floor, or roughly two months of your salary, whichever is greater.
- A typical high-end offer is around four times the statutory floor, or six months of your salary, whichever is greater.
- Where there is a credible discrimination, whistleblowing, or other unfair treatment angle, the high end can stretch to twelve to eighteen months of salary or more, because tribunal awards in those cases are uncapped.

That is the range a good calculator estimates. Your actual offer can sit anywhere in it depending on the circumstances.

How the £30,000 tax rule plays out in practice

The tax position matters because gross figures and net figures can be very different.

- Statutory redundancy pay always sits inside the £30,000 tax-free allowance.
- Ex gratia compensation for loss of employment also sits inside the £30,000 allowance.
- Notice pay (PILON) is fully taxable. It does not benefit from the £30,000 rule.
- Unpaid wages, bonus, and commission are fully taxable as normal earnings.

So if your offer is broken into a tax-free element and a taxable element, the headline figure on the agreement is not what lands in your bank. A solicitor will check that the split is accurate and that nothing taxable has been mislabelled as tax-free.

What pushes a settlement offer up

Several factors push a fair offer towards the high end of the range:

- Long service with the same employer
- A credible discrimination or whistleblowing angle
- Weak performance evidence on the employer's side
- A history of unfair treatment that could be raised at tribunal
- An employer who wants the matter closed quickly and quietly
- Senior or specialist roles where tribunal exposure is greater

What pushes a settlement offer down

Other factors tend to bring the offer towards the low end:

- Short service (less than two years, where unfair dismissal protection does not apply)
- A genuine and well-documented redundancy with a fair selection process
- Documented performance issues with capability or disciplinary records in place
- A mutual termination where you are also the one stepping back

A worked example

Consider an employee with the following facts:

- Salary: £45,000 per year
- Length of service: eight completed years
- Age: 38
- Reason: redundancy
- Contractual notice: three months

The statutory floor breaks down as follows:

- Weekly pay: £45,000 / 52 = £865.38, capped at £719 for redundancy pay
- Redundancy pay: 8 years all in the 22 to 40 multiplier band, so 8 × 1.0 × £719 = £5,752
- Notice pay: contractual three months at full salary = £11,250
- Holiday pay and outstanding wages: not yet calculated, assume zero for this example

Statutory floor: £5,752 + £11,250 = £17,002.

Typical low-end ex gratia: max(£17,002 × 1.5, £45,000 × 2/12) = £25,503.
Typical high-end ex gratia: max(£17,002 × 4, £45,000 × 6/12) = £68,008.

So a fair total settlement for this employee sits roughly between £25,500 and £68,000, depending on the strength of any underlying treatment claims and the employer's appetite to settle.

Of that figure, £30,000 of the ex gratia element is tax-free. The notice pay and anything above £30,000 is taxable as income.

The 10-day Acas consideration period

Your employer is required to give you reasonable time to consider a settlement offer. The Acas Code of Practice on Settlement Agreements sets the minimum at ten calendar days.

This is not a target. It is a floor. If your employer is pressuring you to sign earlier, you can decline and use the full ten days, or longer if needed.

Use that time to:

- Calculate your offer against the statutory floor
- Get independent legal advice from a solicitor
- Decide if you want to negotiate

Who pays for legal advice

Section 203 of the Employment Rights Act 1996 makes a settlement agreement legally binding only if you have received independent legal advice from a qualified adviser.

In practice, your employer almost always pays for this. The standard contribution is £350 to £750 plus VAT for a straightforward settlement agreement review. This applies whether you use the solicitor your employer suggests or a solicitor of your own choosing.

You are not required to use the solicitor your employer recommends. You can pick your own, and the contribution is the same.

The limits of any calculator

A settlement calculator can do a lot. It can show you the statutory floor, estimate a fair range, and tell you whether your offer falls inside or outside that range.

It cannot do these things:

- Assess the legal strength of a discrimination or whistleblowing argument
- Read the specific clauses in your employer's draft agreement
- Account for confidentiality, restrictive covenants, or references being part of the negotiation
- Decide whether to accept, reject, or counter

Those questions need a solicitor. The calculator gives you the numbers. The solicitor advises you on what to do with them.

Frequently asked questions

Is the calculator's figure what I would get at tribunal?
No. A calculator estimates a fair settlement range based on statutory entitlements and market practice. Tribunal awards depend on the specific facts, the strength of evidence, and the panel. They can be higher or lower than a typical settlement.

What if my offer is below the statutory floor?
That is a strong signal that the offer is unreasonable. Your employer cannot offer less than the statutory minimum on redundancy or notice pay. If they have, raise this with your solicitor as the first point of negotiation.

Does the £30,000 tax-free rule apply to all of my settlement?
No. It only applies to the genuine ex gratia compensation element and statutory redundancy pay. Notice pay, wages, holiday pay, bonus, and commission are all taxable as normal earnings. Make sure the agreement separates the elements correctly.

Can I negotiate after I have used the calculator?
Yes. The calculator gives you a fact-based starting point. Many settlement agreements are negotiated upwards once the employee gets independent legal advice. Your solicitor will tell you whether your specific situation supports a counter-offer.

Do I have to take the calculator's range as fact?
No. Use it as a benchmark. Every settlement is different. A calculator cannot weigh up the legal strength of an underlying claim, the employer's appetite to settle, or other commercial factors. It is one input into an informed decision.

How long does the whole process take?
A settlement agreement typically moves from offer to signed document in two to four weeks. That includes the Acas ten-day consideration period and the time it takes for your solicitor to review the agreement and advise you. Faster is possible if both sides are aligned.

What happens after I sign?
The settlement agreement becomes legally binding once you and your employer have signed and your solicitor has signed the certificate confirming you received independent advice. The payment is then made on the date set out in the agreement, often on or shortly after your termination date.
